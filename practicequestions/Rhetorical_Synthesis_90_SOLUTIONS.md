# DOC 2 — SOLUTIONS: Rhetorical Synthesis (Notes → Goal) — 90 Questions

**Universal method for this skill:** (1) Read the GOAL sentence first — underline the exact task verb ("explain," "emphasize a difference," "define," "specify," "give an example," "present the study"). (2) All four options are usually TRUE facts from the notes; only ONE actually *does the goal*. (3) Eliminate options that state a true but off-task fact. The correct answer contains the specific element the goal asks for (the contrast word, the defined term, the number, the two compared items, etc.).

## TOPIC SUMMARY — Rhetorical Synthesis (Notes → Goal)

**Top 3 mistakes students make**
1. **Choosing a true-but-off-task fact.** All four options are usually accurate; the trap is picking a correct detail that doesn't perform the specific goal (e.g., stating "it's expensive" when the goal is "explain *why* it's expensive").
2. **Answering the opposite task.** Selecting a *similarity* when the goal asks for a *difference* (or vice versa). Contrast goals need both items opposed; similarity goals need both items sharing one trait.
3. **Giving half a two-part goal.** For goals like "define AND give an example," "study AND findings," "goal AND result," or "saved water WITHOUT losing yield," students pick a choice that nails only one half.

**Top 3 rules to win every time**
1. **Read the goal sentence first and underline the task verb.** Let it tell you exactly what element to hunt for (a number, a contrast, a definition, a cause, a purpose).
2. **Cause vs. data vs. conclusion.** When a goal asks *why/how/explanation*, choose the causal or mechanism note — NOT the raw numbers (data) and NOT the bare restated outcome. When it asks to *present findings*, you DO need the result.
3. **Match every qualifier.** "In nature," "chemical," "audience already familiar," "during drought," "over X" — each qualifier eliminates otherwise-tempting options. If a choice ignores the qualifier, it's out.

**Expected frequency per module:** Rhetorical Synthesis ("notes → goal") is part of the *Expression of Ideas* domain. Expect **1–2 questions per Reading & Writing module** (so **2–4 per full RW section** across its two modules). Difficulty tends to rise in the harder second module, where more options are all true and the goal draws a finer distinction (cause vs. data, precise vs. vague, one-part vs. two-part).
